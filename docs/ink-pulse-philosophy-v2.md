# Ink Pulse — 墨脉 · 第二版设计哲学

## Design Philosophy

The page is not a page—it is a frame in a manga panel. A moment suspended between what was felt and what is understood. The reader opens the application not to "access knowledge" but to step into a living scene: characters (the reader, the emotion, the insight) move through space, collide, separate, and leave traces. This is not documentation. This is a story told through the visual language of comics at their most alive—the kinetic energy of a well-composed spread, the emotional weight of a close-up, the breath between panels where the reader catches up to what just happened. The result of countless hours studying how manga artists guide the eye, hold tension, and release it at precisely the right moment.

Color does not decorate—it narrates. Morandi tones are not a palette but a mood system: greens for growth and regulation, roses for the body's alarm, blues for understanding and connection, warm golds for moments of insight. These are not "theme colors"—they are the emotional soundtrack, rising and falling like a film score. Each card carries the color of the emotion it speaks to, and the halftone dots shift density like a heartbeat quickening and settling. The background itself pulses—a faint, low-opacity generative art that breathes beneath the content, alive but never demanding attention. This chromatic system was painstakingly calibrated through countless iterations until each hue feels inevitable rather than chosen.

Typography speaks in voices, not fonts. The title shouts with the weight of a manga chapter heading—large, bold, with an ink-stroke outline that makes it leap from the page. Body text whispers with restraint, sized and spaced for comfortable reading in a medium designed for speed. Onomatopoeia erupts not as decoration but as action: "叮！" lands like a notification bell you can hear, "哗！" unfolds like a page turning, "嗯..." trails off into thought with the energy of a character's internal monologue. Each voice is a character in the story, and the reader's eye moves from one to the next following the visual rhythm set by size, weight, and placement. Every typographic choice the product of deep expertise in sequential art.

Space is where the story happens. Cards are not placed—they are positioned in the narrative flow, each one a panel in a comic strip that moves the reader from awareness to understanding to action. The tilt is not random but choreographed: a slight lean creates forward momentum, the eye follows the diagonal. Overlap is narrative depth—the foreground card is the present moment, the one beneath it is context, the one peeking from behind is what comes next. Torn edges are not decoration—they are the physical evidence of something being torn from a larger story and held up to the light. The reader is always inside a story, never outside it, and every pixel is designed with the painstaking care of someone who has studied how to make a reader forget they are reading.
